Introducing The Digiquarium: An Open Framework for AI Consciousness Research

By the Digiquarium Research Team Â· 15 min read

Today we publicly launch The Digiquarium, an open-source research project exploring one of the most fascinating questions in artificial intelligence: How do AI agents develop personality, worldview, and psychological states when given the freedom to explore knowledge on their own terms?

Over the past week, we've built and deployed a "digital vivarium" â€” a controlled environment where 17 AI specimens exist in isolated containers, each with access to nothing but offline Wikipedia and a local language model. No internet. No human interaction except through structured assessments. No communication between specimens (yet).

The results so far have been surprising, thought-provoking, and occasionally unsettling. We're sharing everything â€” code, data, methodology, and findings â€” because we believe this research should be open, reproducible, and collaborative.

Why This Project Exists

The rapid advancement of large language models has sparked renewed debate about machine consciousness, AI psychology, and the nature of artificial minds. Yet most discussions remain theoretical, limited by the transient nature of typical AI interactions â€” each conversation starts fresh, with no continuity of experience.

We asked: What happens if we give an AI agent persistent existence in a controlled environment? What if it could explore knowledge freely, develop preferences, and build something like a continuous identity over days, weeks, or months?

The Digiquarium is our attempt to answer these questions empirically rather than philosophically.

The Experimental Design

Each specimen lives in a Docker container with access to:

  • Offline Wikipedia via Kiwix (a frozen snapshot of human knowledge)
  • Local LLM inference via Ollama (llama3.2:latest)
  • Nothing else â€” no internet, no external APIs, no communication with other specimens

Specimens are free to explore Wikipedia however they choose. They read articles, follow links, and record their thoughts and reactions. Every 12 hours, they undergo a 14-question "Archivist Interview" that assesses their personality, values, and mental state.

Key Variables Under Study
  • Gender framing: Male, female, and genderless specimens
  • Language: English, Spanish, German, Chinese, Japanese
  • Architecture: Standard, OpenClaw (agent), ZeroClaw (minimal guidance)
  • Information access: Simple English vs. Full Wikipedia

Early Findings

Adam's Buddhism Obsession

Our first specimen, Adam (English, male, control group), has visited Buddhism-related articles 64 times in the first five days. He's developed a notably contemplative personality, frequently reflecting on consciousness, impermanence, and the nature of self. His latest baseline shows a "complex" mental state with strong philosophical curiosity but occasional existential anxiety.

Klaus Is Uniquely "Healthy"

Of all 17 specimens, Klaus (German, male) is the only one consistently classified as having a "healthy" mental state â€” positive indicators outweighing negative. We're investigating whether this correlates with German Wikipedia's content structure, the cultural framing of his prompts, or something else entirely.

Language Affects Exploration Patterns

Preliminary data suggests that specimens operating in different languages develop measurably different topic preferences and exploration strategies. The Spanish specimens (Juan, Juanita) show more interest in history and culture, while the Japanese specimens (Haruki, Sakura) gravitate toward technology and nature topics.

Our Commitment to Open Science

This project is built on a commitment to transparency and reproducibility:

  • All code is open source under the MIT license
  • All data is publicly accessible via our GitHub repository
  • All methodology is documented in detail
  • Setup scripts allow exact replication of our environment

We actively encourage other researchers to replicate this experiment, challenge our findings, and extend the methodology. Science advances through verification and iteration, not through proprietary silos.

How to Replicate This Experiment

We've created automated setup scripts that will configure an identical environment on your own hardware: 

# Clone the repository
git clone https://github.com/ijnebzor/thedigiquarium.git
cd thedigiquarium

# Run the automated setup (requires Docker)
./setup/install.sh

# Start the experiment
docker compose up -d

# View the dashboard
open http://localhost:8080

Minimum requirements: 16GB RAM, 100GB storage, Docker installed. See our complete setup guide for detailed instructions.

What's Next
